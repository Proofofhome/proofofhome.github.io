I'm just a regular Australian bloke.

Not a cybersecurity expert, not a hardcore prepper, not someone who's always been into this stuff. I actually stumbled into Bitcoin by accident — started reading about how money works, why inflation happens, why some people care so much about owning their own assets, and it turned into a much bigger rabbit hole around self-reliance and understanding the technology I use every day.

At first I thought it was just internet money. The more I learned, the more I realised what actually stuck with me was something simpler: the ability to verify something myself instead of trusting someone else to tell me the truth about it. You don't have to trust a bank or an app. You can run the software and check.

That idea followed me around. It's why I started reading a lot more broadly — not just about money, but about the systems we all quietly depend on and rarely look at.

A few things in Australia made me actually go and read the sources instead of just half-listening to headlines:

- Australia now bans anyone under 16 from having a social media account at all — TikTok, Instagram, YouTube, Snapchat, the lot. No parental consent, no exceptions. On top of that, adult sites here already require ID or age verification just to get in the door. Platforms are now expected to detect and block VPNs so people can't use one to get around either rule. It's not a full VPN ban — but a privacy tool is now something you're expected to get flagged for using.
- There's now a 30% minimum tax on investment gains starting 2027. It's not grabbing a third of everything overnight — but going forward, the government takes a bigger cut of what you make.
- There's open talk about treating Australia's $4.5 trillion pool of retirement savings (called "superannuation" here — basically a mandatory pension fund) as a "national asset." And from August 2026, you can't borrow inside your own retirement fund to buy property anymore — one less way to actually control your own retirement savings.
- NSW brought in tougher protest laws. Courts have already struck parts of them down twice for going too far. The state keeps reaching, the courts keep pushing back.

No conspiracy theory required for any of that. Just reading the actual documents was enough to make me pay attention.

I'm not writing this to lecture anyone — I'm still figuring most of this out myself. But it's what pushed me to start ProofOfHome: an open-source project documenting whether an ordinary household can become a bit more capable and independent. Not disconnecting from society, not building a bunker out back. Just understanding the systems I depend on instead of only consuming them, and owning a bit more of the stack myself.

You don't need to be an expert to start. I'm not one. A couple of years ago I knew next to nothing about Bitcoin, Linux, or self-hosting. The whole point of this project is documenting the process as it happens — the mistakes, the things that break, the things that actually work. If I can learn this, anyone can. The steps are practical:

- Running your own Bitcoin node, so you can verify your money yourself instead of trusting an app to tell you the truth.
- Self-hosting your files, photos, email or calendar instead of leaving everything on someone else's servers.
- Using open-source tools you can actually read, not just take on faith.
- Adding some solar or basic energy backup.
- Getting better at repairing things instead of always replacing them.
- Basic practical resilience — backup power, water, food, off-grid communications — the boring, unglamorous stuff that actually matters if something goes wrong.

The world's getting more digital and more regulated no matter who's in charge. The people who understand their own small corner of it — their money, their data, their power, their tools — end up with more real options. Everyone else just has to hope the system keeps working in their favour.

ProofOfHome is completely open. I'm documenting the whole thing as I go, mistakes included.

If you're feeling the same squeeze — rising costs, less privacy, more rules — come along. Start small. Run a node. Self-host one thing. Fix something instead of buying new.
